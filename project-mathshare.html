Mathshare: Making Math Accessible, Step by Step

Project Context

Math is a gateway to opportunity-but for millions of students with disabilities, traditional math tools and digital content are barriers rather than bridges. At Benetech, I led UX research for Mathshare: an inclusive, step-by-step math problem-solving platform designed to empower all learners, especially those with learning disabilities, dyslexia, and low vision.

Mathshare enables students to break down math problems, show their work, and explain their reasoning in a way that’s accessible to teachers and compatible with assistive technologies. Our mission: make math not just accessible, but understandable and confidence-building for every student.

My Role

As the lead UX Researcher, I:
  • Drove user research from discovery to validation, collaborating with product, engineering, and education teams
  • Conducted interviews, usability studies, and classroom pilots with students and teachers
  • Synthesized feedback into actionable design recommendations
  • Advocated for accessibility and inclusive design at every stage

Research Approach

To ensure Mathshare truly met the needs of diverse learners, I used a multi-pronged, participatory research strategy:

  • Stakeholder Interviews: Engaged students with disabilities, special education teachers, and assistive technology coordinators to surface pain points and aspirations.
  • Classroom Pilots: Observed Mathshare in real K-12 settings, gathering feedback on usability, accessibility, and learning outcomes.
  • Iterative Usability Testing: Ran remote and in-person sessions to test new features, focusing on keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, and cognitive load.
  • Surveys & Analytics: Collected quantitative data on engagement, task completion, and satisfaction to complement qualitative insights.

Key Insights

  • Step-by-Step Problem Solving: Students benefited from breaking problems into discrete steps, which reduced overwhelm and supported executive function.
  • Show Your Work: The ability to annotate reasoning helped teachers understand student thinking and provided scaffolding for learners who struggled with traditional formats.
  • Accessibility Gaps: Many math tools failed to support screen readers, keyboard navigation, or alternative input methods, creating barriers for students with visual impairments or motor disabilities.
  • Teacher Workflows: Teachers needed simple ways to assign problems, review student work, and provide feedback-all while managing diverse classroom needs.

Impact & Recommendations

Based on these findings, we delivered:

  • An Intuitive, Accessible Editor: Built on top of MathLive, Mathshare’s editor supports keyboard navigation, screen readers, and step-by-step input.
  • Seamless Teacher Integration: Teachers can assign, review, and give feedback on student work, streamlining classroom workflows.
  • Open Source & Free: Mathshare is free for schools and integrates with learning management systems, expanding reach and equity.
  • Born Accessible: Every feature was designed to meet or exceed accessibility standards, ensuring compatibility with assistive technologies.
